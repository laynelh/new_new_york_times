The Death of the Natural Diamond; How This Disappearance is Shaking the World

The last natural diamond was mined from the Earth's surface, what events led up to this moment and what does it mean for our environment.

Aikhal, which used to be the largest natural diamond mine, located in Yakutia, Russia. Source: Mining.com.

By Layne Hunt

September 15, 2176

Natural Diamonds are classified as carbon that has been compressed below the Earth’s mantle for millions of years. The last known natural diamonds were mined by a marine mining vessel off the coast of Namibia on Sunday, September 15, 2176. This event, though inevitable, came as a somber reminder after countless warnings from climate conservation scientists who cautioned that the days of mining these rare gems were numbered.

For centuries, natural diamonds dazzled and mesmerized the masses. Their iconic sparkle graces the jewelry and crowns of the elite while bringing love to millions through the popularization of engagement rings. The stone can be seen making an appearance at almost every exclusive and important event throughout history, appearing in Europe as early as 1074 when they adorned a Hungarian queen’s crown. Over time, they became synonymous with luxury, wealth, and exclusivity, thrusting the mineral into the spotlight of society's rich and powerful. Some notable diamonds of the past include the Hope Diamond, known for its rumored curse, The Cullian diamond located at the helm of the Sovereign's Scepter with Cross, and the Koh-i-Noor, said to be a gift from the gods. Transcending the physical, diamonds ingrained themselves into pop culture through many different types of media including medieval paintings, song, and video content. One instance of this includes the song “Diamonds are a girl's best friend” sung by Marylin Monroee in 1954.

Image of the Scepter of the Cross which contains the Cullian 2 diamond. Source: Historic Royal Palaces

Natural diamonds hold a much longer history than humans, with most forming at least 1 billion years ago amongst temperatures up to 1300 degrees Celsius within the Earth's mantle. In some instances, diamonds can form up to 150 miles into the mantle. It is widely known as the hardest mineral on Earth, even surpassing steel. Most diamonds made their way to the surface through volcanic activity, leaving the mineral scattered throughout ancient lava tubes.

Diamonds were first discovered by humans in India nearly 4,000 years ago. During this time, they were prized for their medicinal properties and did not appear in Europe until 327 BC. Throughout history, radiance and strength warped into an underlying perception of luxury and invincibility. Royals in Europe believed diamonds made them invincible, associating the gem's indestructibility with their own rule. For millennia, diamonds have been endowed with myths and folklore, imbuing them with cultural and symbolic power.

Historically, the diamond trade has contributed significantly to our economies, becoming a pivotal source of revenue for previously war-torn countries with greater access to this resource. Despite these economic contributions, diamond mining is known to have significant environmental impacts including habitat destruction, water pollution, and carbon emissions. Furthermore, the mining industry is a notoriously bad working environment, with some workers getting paid merely 1 dollar a day. In 2001, it was reported that the Diamond and Jewelry business generated an annual 24.1 billion in economic value. As these natural diamonds became less common, this estimate increased to a staggering 120 billion in global exports in the year 2095. Natural diamonds, once abundant enough to be traded freely, were elevated to near-mythical status as their availability waned. We as a species have internalized this scarcity as value, leading to this ballooning of price, an increase in underground trading activity, and a rise in overall criminal activity associated with the stone.

A likely motivator for such growth is the carefully crafted relationship between diamonds and love. The first documented proposal using a diamond engagement ring was in 1477 when Archduke Maximilian of Austria asked Mary of Burgundy to be his wife. Thus, a new precedent was set and began to spread throughout the upper classes of Europe. This tie to love and romance developed further during the mid 20th century when the company De Beers released the slogan “A Diamond is forever,” This slogan, along with other clever marketing tactics, transformed the stone into a staple of the loving relationship and greatly contributed to their rise in popularity.

Tangentially, the contention between lab and natural diamonds maintained a steady pace, beginning with the first lab created diamonds in 1954 by General Energy. During these early years, lab diamonds were considered imitations of their natural counterparts. However, the creation of the first gem-quality lab diamond in 1971 marked a turning point in history and opened the public to lab diamonds as a true substitute for natural diamonds. In recent years, technological advances have made it easier than ever to create such gems , increasing their distribution. This has unintentionally caused their monetary value to plummet, contrasting the seemingly infinite increase natural diamonds are enjoying since this announcement. Beyond their increasing affordability, lab diamonds have a significantly lower carbon footprint and little to no ecological impact. As natural diamond supply comes to a stop, we can expect a corresponding decrease in energy consumption associated with diamond production, further minimizing the environmental impact.

Image of the De Beers factory. Source: Canadian Mining Journal

A gem of the Earth, born in fire and miraculously traveling towards the surface, only to be exploited to the point of extinction by humans. Just as we as a species have consumed without regard for our environment, we depleted and destroyed something we held with such value. Looking toward the future, we can only hope to protect our environment from further destruction, though the loss of the natural diamonds may only be the beginning.
