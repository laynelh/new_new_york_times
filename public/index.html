The Death of Diamonds: How the disappearence of natural diamond's is shaking the world

By Layne Hunt | Published September 15, 2076

Aikhal, which used to be the largest natural diamond mine, located in Yakutia, Russia. Source: Mining.com.

[Summary] Natural Diamonds are classified as carbon that has been compressed below the Earth’s mantle for millions of years. The last known natural diamonds were mined by a marine mining vessel off the coast of Namibia on Sunday, September 15, 2176. This death comes after numerous warnings from chief climate conservation scientists.

[Notable Appearances] However, diamonds are arguably most notable by their characteristic shimmer as they embellish jewelry and accessories. Their brilliance can be seen making an appearance at almost every exclusive and important event throughout history, starting in 1074 where they were present as the centerpiece of BLANK’s crown during the CORONATION. Over time, they became synonymous with luxury, wealth, and exclusivity, thrusting the mineral into the spotlight of society's rich and powerful. One of the largest diamonds ever mined includes the Cullian diamond, positioned at the top of one of the English Monarchy’s Crown Jewels, Sovereign's Sceptre with Cross.

[Strictly Facts (Geology)] Natural diamonds hold a much longer history than humans, with most forming at least 1 billion years ago amongst temperatures up to 1300 degrees Celsius within the Earth’s mantle. In some instances, diamonds can form up to 150 miles into the mantle. It is widely known as the hardest mineral on Earth, surpassing boron. Most diamonds made their way to the surface through volcanic activity, leaving the mineral scattered throughout ancient lava tubes.

[First discovery and History (humans)] Diamonds were first discovered by humans in India nearly 4,000 years ago. During this time, they were prized for their medicinal properties and did not appear in Europe until 327 BC. Over time, the folklore around the mineral increased, leading people to associate their immense strength with invincibility and Royals believed diamonds made them invincible.

[Economy] Historically, the diamond trade has contributed a significant amount to our economies, estimated worth being 123 billion dollars in the year 2022. As this resource became scarcer, prices skyrocketed, leading to a whopping 500 billion estimated worth in just 15 years.

[lab diamonds] The rivalry between lab and natural diamonds has maintained a healthy fire since the first lab created diamond in 1954 by GE. However, the first gem-quality crystals were created a few decades later in 1971. For much of their history, lab diamonds have been viewed as an imitation of their natural counterparts. Due to recent technological advances that make it easier than ever to create such gems, lab diamond’s value has dramatically plummeted, contrasting the seemingly infinite increase natural diamonds are enjoying since this announcement.
